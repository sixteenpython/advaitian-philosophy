# Chapter 1 — The Problem as Art Object

> *A solved problem is consumed once. A designed problem teaches forever.*

---

## §0 — Opening: The Architecture School That Did Not Build

An architect — call him an architect; the dream does not quite say what he was — wakes from a terrible nightmare. In his dream he finds himself in a country where architecture education has become mandatory and universal. *"We are preparing our students for the built environment of the future,"* the educators say. *"They must speak the language of buildings."* Studies are commissioned. Committees are formed. Curricula are written. All of this is done without consulting a single working architect.

Since architects are known to record their ideas on paper — on blueprints — these blueprints must constitute the language of architecture. It is imperative that students become fluent in this language; indeed, it would be ludicrous to expect a child to design a room or sketch a façade without a thorough grounding in plan-reading and structural notation. Sketching, designing, actually proposing a building — these are advanced topics, generally reserved for graduate school.

In the primary and secondary schools, then, the mission is to train students to *use* this language. Architecture class is where the teacher draws a floor plan on the board and the students copy it, or rotate it ninety degrees, or convert it from imperial to metric. There are quizzes on the names of structural elements. There are tests on load-path identification. The students learn the difference between a cornice and a soffit. Top students memorise the building codes by heart and can recite the relevant clauses in three seconds flat. They are praised. *"He has real architectural ability,"* the teachers say. *"Look how neatly he labels the columns."*

In the higher grades, the pressure intensifies. Students must take Plan Reading, Code Compliance, Specification Drafting, and Cost Estimation. *"It's a lot for them,"* the teachers admit, *"but later, when they finally encounter a real building, they'll appreciate all the preparation."* Of course, not many students go on to encounter real buildings. Most graduate, take office jobs, and remember architecture only as the subject in which they once scored fifty-three percent and were told they were not creative enough.

The architect, in his dream, asks the teacher: *"And when do students get to actually design something?"*

*"Design?"* the teacher repeats, blinking. *"Well, design is for the senior partners at the big firms. We don't expect a child to design. We teach them what design is — they read about it in their textbooks — but actually proposing a building, drawing one's own plan, choosing where the windows go… that requires a degree of maturity and judgement that comes only after many years. We have to give them the *foundation* first."*

*"And how many of them ever get to the senior-partner level?"*

The teacher waves the question away. *"That depends on the student."*

The architect walks out of the school into the dream-city, and finds it gridded out in rectangles of grey concrete. Every building is identical. No one knows how it could be otherwise. *No one has ever been taught to ask.*

He wakes in a cold sweat, gratefully. *Of course not,* he tells himself. *No society would do this to architecture. We understand that buildings are designed. The whole culture knows it. There are museums. There are Pritzker Prizes. There are coffee-table books. Architecture is a creative practice and everybody knows it, even the people who do not practise it.*

He gets up, makes coffee, and reads the newspaper. A column on the editorial page is complaining about declining mathematics scores.

Vikram Sundaresan finishes his fourth year at IIT Bombay in May 2024 with a CGPA of 9.4 and a placement offer from a top-tier quantitative-trading firm.[^vikram] In the six years between his Class XI tutorial-academy enrolment and his final-year placement interview, he has solved — at a rough estimate — eighteen thousand mathematics problems. He has solved problems from textbooks, from coaching-class problem sets, from JEE Advanced past papers, from RMO, from INMO, from the *Educative JEE* volumes, from his coursework, from his summer-internship pre-reading, from his interview prep. He is, by any reasonable measure, one of the strongest mathematical problem-solvers his city has produced in his cohort.

He has designed exactly zero problems.

It has not occurred to him that this is strange. It has not occurred to his teachers, his tutors, his interview coaches, or his interview panel that this is strange. The pipeline from school to placement, in his experience, has only one direction: a problem is set, a problem is solved, the solver is graded. The arrow goes one way. The student is forever the recipient of problems and never the producer of them. The act of producing — of choosing what the problem should be, of building the architecture so that a particular insight will emerge — is a separate craft, practised somewhere else, by someone else, for reasons the students are not told.

This is the architecture school I just told you about. We are inside it. We have always been inside it.

The book you are reading is a key to the door.

## §1 — The Argument

### §1.1 The blind spot in mathematics education

Every mathematician knows that a problem is designed by someone. The IMO problem committee meets, deliberates, selects, calibrates. The Putnam committee does likewise. The JEE Advanced paper-setters do likewise. K.D. Joshi, when he wrote the *Educative JEE Mathematics* volumes, designed every exercise in them. George Polya designed every example in *How to Solve It*. Arthur Engel designed every numbered exercise in *Problem-Solving Strategies*. Terence Tao designed (or curated, with substantial design choice in the curation) every problem in *Solving Mathematical Problems*.

The asymmetry is striking. **Every mathematics book the student reads was written by a designer of problems. None of those books teaches the student to become one.**

This is not because the design of problems is some occult art forbidden to outsiders. It is not. Problem design is a learnable discipline with its own moves, its own constraints, and its own canon of past masters. Its absence from the standard curriculum is not pedagogical necessity; it is pedagogical accident. The Indian mathematics curriculum, at every level from board exams to JEE to RMO to graduate qualifying exams, treats the student as a *consumer* of problems. The Western curriculum is, on this specific point, scarcely better. Lockhart's *Lament* makes the larger case — that mathematics is taught as a sterile set of procedures rather than as a creative art — but Lockhart's argument applies with particular force to the production side of mathematical practice. We do not teach our students to make problems because we do not, ourselves, *think of problems as having been made*.

This is the central blind spot. Once it is named, it cannot be unnamed.

A designed problem has all the features of a designed object. It has a central animating idea (the *CEP*, the Central Elegant Point). It has a chosen surface presentation (the *statement-craft*). It has a calibrated difficulty (the *concealment depth*). It has anticipated wrong paths (the *traps*). It has a beauty that comes from the fit of these parts to one another. None of these features are accidents of nature, the way the value of $\pi$ is an accident of nature. They are the marks of a maker.

The maker, in most cases, was a mathematician with a particular taste and a particular intent. The student, encountering the problem in cold print, is asked to *reverse-engineer* the maker's design without ever being told the design exists. This is like asking a literature student to analyse a poem without ever telling them that the poem was written by a person who chose every word.

Pillar 4 of this book makes the maker visible.

### §1.2 The historical case: from Polya to Lockhart to Tao

The argument I am making is not new. It has been made, in fragments and gestures and full-throated assertions, by every major mathematician-pedagogue of the past century. What is new — what I think is new — is the *systematic* construction of a curriculum around it.

George Polya, in *How to Solve It* (1945), wrote a book ostensibly aimed at the solver. But Polya was also, throughout, writing for the teacher and the problem-setter; he understood that *the way a problem is asked* is at least as consequential as *what is asked*. His Part III, the "Short Dictionary of Heuristic," contains entries like "Could you restate the problem?", "Inventor's Paradox," and "Variation of the problem" — all of which are, when read carefully, instructions for the *designer* as much as for the solver. The Polya whose four-phase loop ("understand → plan → execute → look back") gave the world its first systematic pedagogy of problem-solving understood from the start that the solver and the designer are two faces of the same craftsman, and that the work of one informs the work of the other.

Polya's framework, four-phase though it is, becomes a designer's framework when you read it from the other side of the desk. Phase 1 ("understand") becomes "make sure the surface is unambiguous." Phase 2 ("devise a plan") becomes "calibrate the difficulty by choosing how many archetypes the solver must hold simultaneously." Phase 3 ("carry out") becomes "ensure the solver's intended path is actually traversable from the stated premises." Phase 4 ("look back") becomes the designer's most consequential phase: stress-test the design against alternative paths, against accidental degeneracies, against the trivialisation by some clever shortcut you did not anticipate. We will return to this Polya inversion in Chapter 3, where it becomes the structural backbone of the 5-Step CEP Design Framework.

Paul Lockhart, in *A Mathematician's Lament* (2002 essay; 2009 book), made the polemical case I have already echoed in §0. Lockhart writes from the position of a research mathematician who, having returned to K-12 teaching, was appalled at what mathematics education had become. *"Mathematics is an art,"* he insists. *"The art is not in the 'truth' but in the explanation, the argument. Mathematics is the art of explanation."*[^lockhart-art] If this is so — and I think it is so — then problem design is the most explicit form of mathematical art-making. Every designed problem is an *invitation* to the reader to discover an explanation that the designer has already conceived. To design a problem is to make a small museum out of one mathematical idea.

[^lockhart-art]: Lockhart, *A Mathematician's Lament*, expanded 2009 edition (Bellevue Literary Press), p. 5 of the source PDF. The full sentence: *"By concentrating on what, and leaving out why, mathematics is reduced to an empty shell. The art is not in the 'truth' but in the explanation, the argument. It is the argument itself which gives the truth its context, and determines what is really being said and meant. Mathematics is the art of explanation."* Cf. `Cursor-Lockhart.md` §III Sample 4.

Terence Tao, in *Solving Mathematical Problems* (2006), did something subtler. He took an entire book of olympiad-level problems and walked the reader through his own *think-aloud* on each one — including, crucially, his own *abandoned* approaches and his reasons for abandoning them. Tao's nine-strategy framework (which we will study as the solver-side foil to our 5-step design framework in Chapter 3) is *the* contemporary expression of the Polya tradition. But what Tao did that Polya did not is even more important: Tao made the solver's *internal monologue* visible. He showed that the problem-solving mind is constantly hypothesising, testing, retreating, re-attempting. Once you have read Tao on the inside of the solver's mind, you cannot help but begin to wonder about the *inside of the designer's mind*. You begin to ask: how did the problem-setter know that *this* would be the elegant path? What internal monologue did *they* have, when they decided to set the problem this way and not another? Pillar 4 is the answer to that question.

These three — Polya, Lockhart, Tao — form the genealogical line into which this pillar plants itself. The line is real. Anyone who has read all three will recognise that they are pointing, from three different angles, at the same gap in our culture. Pillar 4 is the attempt to fill that gap.

### §1.3 What Pillar 4 will do

The pillar that begins with this chapter has nine chapters and three commitments.

The first commitment is **descriptive**: in Chapters 4 through 8, the pillar dissects twenty-five real problems — twenty-two from the International Mathematical Olympiad, two from the Putnam, one from K.D. Joshi's *Educative JEE Mathematics* — as case studies in design. Each case study answers four questions about the problem on the page: *What was the designer's CEP — the central mathematical insight the problem was built around? Which archetypes did the designer choose, and why these and not others? What traps did the designer plant, and what makes each trap pedagogically generous rather than punishing? What does the surface statement reveal, and what does it deliberately conceal?* The twenty-five cases ascend in difficulty from the cleanest Moderate-tier exemplar (IMO 1959 P1, which we will meet again in Chapter 2 as a fully-worked anatomical specimen) to the legendary IMO 1988 P6 (the capstone of Chapter 8, and the most consequential designed problem of the past half-century).

The second commitment is **prescriptive**: in Chapter 3, the pillar formalises a 5-Step CEP Design Framework — the inversion of Polya's four-phase solver-loop into a five-step designer-loop. By the end of Chapter 3 the reader has a usable, named procedure for designing problems of their own. The framework is not magic. Like Polya's framework, it does not guarantee that the procedure will produce a beautiful problem on the first attempt. What it does guarantee is that the procedure makes the designer's choices *legible* — to themselves and to others — so that what was once an opaque act of taste becomes a craft with named moves and named trade-offs.

The third commitment is **participatory**: in Chapter 9, the pillar puts the framework into the reader's hands. Three guided design exercises — each given a CEP and a target archetype profile — invite the reader to design a problem and then assess it against the Designer's Rubric. By the end of Chapter 9 the reader has done what no Indian mathematics curriculum has previously asked them to do: they have *made* a problem, however small, and they have a vocabulary for judging whether they made it well.

If the pillar succeeds, the reader will close it with a different relationship to every mathematics problem they encounter for the rest of their life. They will see, in each one, the marks of the maker.

## §2 — Worked Demonstration: IMO 1988 Problem 6 as an Aesthetic Object

I am not going to solve IMO 1988 Problem 6 in this chapter. The full case-study treatment — including the multi-archetype convergence analysis and the four-step designer's reconstruction — is the capstone of Chapter 8, where it occupies pride of place as Case 25, the most consequential single problem in the slate. What I want to do here is something smaller and (I think) more important for the chapter's purposes: I want to ask, of the problem itself, *what kind of object it is*.

The problem, in its IMO-Compendium-verified form:[^imo88p6]

> Let $a$ and $b$ be two positive integers such that $ab + 1$ divides $a^2 + b^2$. Show that $(a^2 + b^2)/(ab + 1)$ is a perfect square.

[^imo88p6]: *The IMO Compendium*, Djukić, Janković, Matić, Petrović (Springer 2006), problem statement at `imo-compendium.txt` line 9997. Solution in §4.29 (page 500 of the book).

Notice, first, how short the problem is. Two sentences. No diagram. No specialised vocabulary beyond "positive integers," "divides," and "perfect square" — every undergraduate, every advanced high-schooler, every JEE Advanced aspirant has these concepts.

Notice, second, the asymmetry of the statement. The condition is *given* (the divisibility holds); the conclusion is the *consequence* (the quotient is a perfect square). The problem does *not* say "find all pairs $(a, b)$ such that…" — it does not ask the solver to enumerate, classify, or construct. It asks for a *property*. The asked-for property is, on its face, surprising. There is no obvious reason that a quotient of two general expressions in $a$ and $b$ should be a perfect square; perfect squares are scarce among the natural numbers; the conclusion has, in advance, an air of cleanness that is not warranted by the surface.

This is the **first design choice** to notice. The designer of IMO 1988 P6 chose a *clean* answer — perfect square — and then engineered a surface presentation that conceals which perfect square it is. *The choice of the answer-type is itself the elegance.* A problem whose answer is "some integer," or "a rational number," or "a complex algebraic expression," would not have the same aesthetic effect. The designer's first move was to pick an answer that is, in advance, beautiful.

Notice, third, the structure of the divisibility condition. The form $ab + 1 \mid a^2 + b^2$ is *not* a natural object in elementary number theory. The expression $a^2 + b^2$ is natural (it is the squared length of a vector); the expression $ab + 1$ is natural separately (it is one more than a product). But the *combination* — a divisibility relation between these two specific bilinears — is not natural. It is constructed. The designer chose this specific divisibility relation because, *under it*, the quotient lands on a perfect square; under a slightly different relation (say, $ab + 2 \mid a^2 + b^2$, or $ab + 1 \mid a^2 - b^2$), the conclusion would either be false or would require a different and less elegant proof. **The exact form of the constraint is calibrated to produce the desired conclusion.** This is the **second design choice**.

Notice, fourth, that the proof technique — the famous *Vieta jumping* — is not discoverable from the surface at all. If you have not encountered Vieta jumping before, the problem looks like a number-theory exercise; you would try modular arithmetic, you would try factoring, you would try inequalities. None of these standard approaches close the problem. The technique that does close it requires you to *fix* the quotient $k = (a^2 + b^2)/(ab + 1)$, treat the equation $a^2 + b^2 = k(ab + 1)$ as a quadratic in $a$ for fixed $b$ and $k$, and use Vieta's formulas to extract a second integer solution $a'$ that is *smaller* than $a$ in a precise sense, then iterate this descent until you reach a minimal pair from which the perfect-square conclusion is forced. This is a beautiful technique. It is also a *specific* technique that exists in a tiny niche of olympiad lore. **The designer chose a problem that *requires* a technique most solvers will not have in their toolkit.** This is the **third design choice**.

Notice, fifth — and this is the one that makes the problem legendary — that the chosen technique, once discovered, *generalises*. Vieta jumping is now a named method that solves an entire family of Diophantine problems (IMO 1994 P4 is its most direct cousin; we will meet that one in Chapter 7). Before IMO 1988, Vieta jumping was a folklore trick known to a small circle of problem-setters; after IMO 1988, it entered the mainstream olympiad canon. **The problem did not just demonstrate a beautiful piece of mathematics; it created a new public vocabulary.** This is the **fourth design choice**, and it is the rarest. Most problems exhibit a technique; very few *create* a technique by making it visible to a wide audience for the first time. IMO 1988 P6 did the latter.

Take these four observations together. The designer of this problem chose a clean answer-type (perfect square), engineered a specific constraint to produce it ($ab + 1 \mid a^2 + b^2$ and not some other divisor relation), forced a particular obscure technique (Vieta jumping), and in doing so made that technique part of the language. Each choice is independent. Each choice is a designer's move. None of these moves had to happen; the problem-setter could have chosen otherwise; and the resulting problem would have been either trivial or ugly.

This is what I mean when I say a problem is a designed object. The Vieta-jumping orbit, viewed from inside the proof, is a piece of mathematics — a structural fact about Diophantine quadratics under integer constraints. The orbit, viewed from outside the proof, in conjunction with the problem statement, is a piece of *art* — a deliberate construction by a human author whose intent was to make the orbit visible to a generation of problem-solvers via a single eight-word sentence.

Chapter 8 will return to this problem and walk through the multi-archetype convergence on which the technique rests (#16 Reverse Engineering + #18 Induction + #1 Invariance + #12 Extremal — the only 4-archetype problem in the entire slate, and the highest-difficulty case in Pillar 4). The Pillar 3 reader will recognise the Vieta-jumping treatment from Pillar 3 Chapter 3, where we read the same problem from the *solver's* side. The Chapter 8 treatment will be the *designer's* side. The two together — solver's convergence diagram and designer's reconstruction — give the most complete reading of this problem that I know how to give.

For now, in this chapter, all I want is for the reader to walk away with the feeling that has been latent since the first paragraph of §0: *somebody made this*. Not just in the sense that some IMO problem-setter wrote it down. In the deeper sense that every choice in the problem — the bilinears, the divisibility, the asked-for property, the technique that is needed — was a deliberate craft choice that could have been made otherwise. The problem is an art object. It deserves to be read as one.

## §3 — The Counter-Argument

I anticipate two objections to everything I have just said. I want to give both of them their full force, in the form they actually arrive in conversation, before responding. The form I have chosen is the one Galileo used in *Two New Sciences* and Lockhart used throughout *A Mathematician's Lament* — a dialogue between SIMPLICIO, who voices the objection in good faith, and SALVIATI, who answers from the chapter's commitments. The form is borrowed, deliberately, because borrowed forms carry their own seriousness.

**SIMPLICIO.** Anand, I have read what you have written and I think the central problem with it is straightforward. I am a student. I will not, in any plausible future, be invited to set an IMO problem or to write an *Educative JEE Mathematics* volume. The people who do this work — the Polyas, the Joshis, the IMO committees — are specialists. Why should I, who am training to be a competent solver, spend thirty hours of my reading life learning to *design* problems that I will never be asked to design?

**SALVIATI.** The framing of your question carries the assumption I am trying to challenge. You are asking what *use* design literacy will be to a solver. I want to suggest that the question itself comes from inside the architecture school. From inside the school it is obvious that solver and designer are different roles; the curriculum has trained you to see them as different roles; the entire pipeline from coaching class to placement reinforces it. Step outside the school for a moment. In every other creative discipline you know — literature, music, painting, architecture itself — we *do not* separate the producer from the consumer the way mathematics education does. A literature student reads novels *and* writes essays; a music student listens to recitals *and* takes lessons in composition; an art student visits the gallery *and* puts paint on canvas. Production and consumption are recognised as two faces of the same education. Mathematics, alone among the arts, has decided that the consumer track and the producer track are separable, and has reserved the producer track for an unmarked priesthood. The Polya inversion — the recognition that solver and designer are mirror crafts — is the small unwinding of that separation.

**SIMPLICIO.** That is rhetoric. Give me the practical case. If I spend the thirty hours, what do I get?

**SALVIATI.** Three things. First, you become a better solver. This is the empirical claim and I want to defend it. The mechanical solver who has never thought about design tends to attack every problem with the same toolkit and to be surprised when the toolkit fails; the design-literate solver recognises the *signature* of a problem — what kind of CEP it is built around, which archetype convergence the designer was targeting — and *predicts* which tools will work because they have learned to read the designer's intent. This is the same phenomenon by which a person who has written a sonnet reads other sonnets differently. They see the choices.

**SIMPLICIO.** Second?

**SALVIATI.** Second, you stop misattributing difficulty. A problem you cannot solve, you tend to attribute to your own deficiency: *I am not clever enough; I have not seen this technique; the problem-setter was a genius and I am not.* Sometimes that attribution is right. Often it is wrong. The problem is hard because the designer made it hard, by *specific design choices*, and once you can name those choices the difficulty stops being a fact about you and starts being a fact about the problem. This is liberation. The student who can name the designer's moves is no longer humiliated by problems; they are, instead, in conversation with the designer.

**SIMPLICIO.** And third?

**SALVIATI.** Third — and this is the one I think matters most, though it sounds the most romantic — you join the lineage. The Polyas and the Joshis and the IMO committees are not a priesthood, even if the curriculum has framed them that way. They are mathematicians who decided, at some point in their lives, that they wanted to *make* problems as well as to *solve* them, and who learned how. The threshold of joining that lineage is far lower than the institutional packaging suggests. Pillar 4 is a deliberate attempt to lower it further. The end-of-pillar exercises in Chapter 9 are designed specifically so that any reader who has worked through the case studies can produce a designed problem of their own. Whether anyone ever publishes that problem is beside the point. The point is that you will have done the work that the priesthood does, and you will see — from inside, by direct experience — that the work is not hidden behind any door that requires a key you cannot get.

**SIMPLICIO.** I notice you have not addressed my actual objection, which is about utility.

**SALVIATI.** I have not. I do not think utility is the right frame for this argument. Lockhart's analogue: nobody objects to a poetry class on the grounds that the student will not earn a living as a poet. We accept poetry-writing as part of being a literate human being. I am asking for the same dispensation for problem design — not because every reader will earn a living as a problem-setter, but because the literacy of design is part of what it means to be a mathematically literate human being. The architecture school does not exist to train senior partners; it exists, when it works, to train people who can think about buildings.

**SIMPLICIO.** I do not yet agree.

**SALVIATI.** I do not yet expect you to. I expect you to suspend the objection long enough to work through Chapters 2 through 8, and then I expect you to come back to this dialogue with the case studies in mind. The case studies are the argument. The dialogue is only the announcement that the argument is coming.

## §4 — Bridge to Chapter 2

The chapter you have just read has made one claim repeatedly, in four registers: a problem is a designed object. The architecture-school nightmare of §0 makes the claim emotionally. The historical argument of §1.2 makes it genealogically (Polya, Lockhart, Tao all said versions of it). The IMO 1988 P6 reading of §2 makes it concretely — pointing at four specific design choices in one specific problem and inviting the reader to *see* the maker behind them. The dialogue of §3 makes it argumentatively, by engaging the strongest objection.

What this chapter has *not* done is develop the analytical vocabulary required to read every designed problem in the same way. We have, in §2, named four design choices in IMO 1988 P6 — the clean answer-type, the calibrated constraint, the forced technique, the language-creating effect — but we have not generalised these into a stable anatomy. We do not yet have, as analysts, a list of *the parts a designed problem has*, with names for each part, such that any problem can be dissected along the same axes.

That anatomy is the work of Chapter 2. In *Anatomy of a Well-Designed Problem*, we take a single, moderate-difficulty problem — IMO 1959 Problem 1, the cleanest problem in the entire 1959-2024 IMO archive — and we dissect it along the four anatomical axes that will recur in every Pillar 4 case study: the **Central Elegant Point**, the **archetype convergence**, the **planted traps**, and the **statement-craft**. By the end of Chapter 2 the reader has the vocabulary. By the end of Chapter 3 — which inverts Polya's four-phase solver-loop into a five-step designer-loop — the reader has the *procedure*. By the end of Chapter 8 — twenty-five case studies later — the reader has the *examples*. By the end of Chapter 9 — three guided design exercises later — the reader has *done it themselves*.

We begin the anatomy now.
