# Chapter 8 — Practice Set + Diagnostic

*The diagnostic that tells you whether the toolkit has become reflex.*

---

## §0 Opening Vignette — Dr. Meera Chakraborty grades the camp

It is a Saturday evening in May at the Homi Bhabha Centre for Science Education (HBCSE), Mumbai. The Indian IMO training camp has just completed its third week. Dr. Meera Chakraborty, who has evaluated INMO scripts for the last fifteen years and serves on the Indian IMO team selection committee, is sitting in the third-floor faculty room with thirty answer scripts spread across a long table. Each script is a student's attempt at a 10-problem diagnostic she designed specifically for this camp. The diagnostic, the students were told, *is not for selection* — selection happens on a different exam — *the diagnostic is for self-knowledge*.

Meera evaluates each script not by *score* but by a five-dimensional **profile**. For each student and each problem, she records:

1. **Did the student name the correct archetype-shortlist in the first 60 seconds?** (yes/no)
2. **Did the student draw a usable MVC for 3-archetype problems?** (yes/no/N-A)
3. **Did the student complete within the benchmark time?** (yes/no)
4. **When stuck, did the student invoke a named escape-hatch move, or grind?** (escape / grind)
5. **Across the 10 problems, how many of Pillar 2's 20 archetypes did the student invoke?** (count)

By 10:00 p.m. Meera has graded all thirty scripts. Two patterns emerge:

*The high-score-low-profile students.* Several students scored 7 or 8 out of 10 — high by any measure — but their profile is concerning: they correctly identified the archetype-shortlist on only 4 of 10 problems. The other problems they solved by *pattern-matching* to memorised case studies and got lucky. Their cross-archetype reach (Dimension 5) is narrow — 6 or 7 archetypes across the whole 10. These students will hit a wall on novel problems where pattern-matching fails.

*The low-score-high-profile students.* Several students scored only 5 or 6 — middling — but their profile is excellent: 8 of 10 archetype-shortlists correctly named; usable MVCs on both 3-archetype problems; explicit escape-hatch invocations (in two cases the named move was the Pivot Shadow — a Pillar 3 sophistication Meera does not see often). Their cross-archetype reach is 13 or 14. These students are *closer* to IMO-level mastery than the high-score-low-profile students; they have internalised the Pillar 3 discipline but have not yet ironed out the algebra-and-tactical execution at the level of the high-scorers. With six more months of deliberate practice, they will catch up on execution and the profile-vs-score gap will close.

When Meera meets the cohort the next morning at 9:00 a.m., she does not announce scores. She projects on the screen each student's profile, with the score in a small font in the corner. The first slide reads:

> *Your score is the least interesting thing on this slide.*
> *Your profile is the diagnostic.*
> *Train what your profile says is weakest.*

The students stare at the slides for a long time. The high-score-low-profile students are surprised that their high score is not the headline. The low-score-high-profile students are surprised that their profile is the cause for optimism. Both groups leave the session knowing exactly what to train next.

**This is the chapter that operationalises self-diagnosis.**

## §1 The Pattern

### §1.1 The diagnostic instrument

Chapter 8's central deliverable is the 10-problem **Pillar 3 Diagnostic Set** — a calibrated practice instrument designed to expose, per-problem, which dimensions of the Pillar 3 discipline the reader has internalised and which dimensions need more training. The set is presented in §4 below. Before presenting it, we develop the rubric that turns the 10 attempted solutions into a structured profile.

The diagnostic is **not** an examination. It does not produce a binary pass/fail nor a single score. It produces a *profile* — a five-dimensional vector that describes the reader's current state of internalisation. Two readers with the same total score may have entirely different profiles; the profiles dictate different training regimes.

### §1.2 The five-dimension rubric

For each of the 10 diagnostic problems, the reader records the following five items immediately upon completing (or abandoning) the problem:

| # | Dimension | Question | Answer format |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Protocol fluency** | Did the First-Minute Protocol Step 3 archetype-shortlist contain the actually-used archetype(s)? | Y/N |
| 2 | **MVC accuracy** | Was the MVC's convergence-node label correct (for 3-archetype problems)? | Y/N/N-A |
| 3 | **Time discipline** | Did you complete within the per-problem benchmark (varies by problem; given below)? | Y/N |
| 4 | **Escape-hatch usage** | When stuck (≥ 10 minutes), did you invoke a *named* escape-hatch move (Nudge / Direction Map / Pivot Shadow), or did you grind? | Escape / Grind / Did-not-stall |
| 5 | **Cross-archetype reach** | (Aggregated across all 10 problems) How many distinct Pillar 2 archetypes (out of 20) did you invoke? | Integer 0–20 |

The five dimensions are *independent measurements*: a single problem produces 4 individual measurements (D1–D4) plus a contribution to the aggregate D5.

### §1.3 The benchmark times

Each problem has a per-problem benchmark time, calibrated by archetype-count and difficulty tier:

| Tier | Archetype count | Benchmark time |
|---|---|---|
| Easy (T2) | 2-archetype | 12 minutes |
| Medium (T3) | 2-to-3 borderline | 20 minutes |
| Hard (T4) | 3-archetype | 30 minutes |

A reader who solves a Tier-T4 problem in 18 minutes scores Y on Dimension 3 for that problem. A reader who solves it in 35 minutes scores N — *even if the solution is correct*. The benchmark is not about correctness; it is about *time-discipline*, which is a Pillar 3 skill in its own right.

### §1.4 The diagnostic profile and what to train

After completing the 10-problem set, the reader has 10 D1 measurements, 10 D2 measurements, 10 D3 measurements, 10 D4 measurements, and 1 aggregate D5. The diagnostic profile is summarised as:

```
PROFILE = (D1_score, D2_score, D3_score, D4_stalls_with_escape, D5_count)
```

where:
- **D1_score** = number of problems with Y on Dimension 1 (max 10)
- **D2_score** = number of problems with Y on Dimension 2 (max equal to the number of 3-archetype problems in the set, which is 2)
- **D3_score** = number of problems completed within benchmark (max 10)
- **D4_stalls_with_escape** = of the problems where the reader stalled, the fraction where a named escape-hatch was invoked (e.g., 3/4 = 75%)
- **D5_count** = distinct Pillar 2 archetypes invoked across the 10 problems (max 20)

The *target* profile for an INMO-ready Pillar 3 solver is approximately:

```
TARGET = (D1 ≥ 8/10, D2 ≥ 2/2, D3 ≥ 7/10, D4 ≥ 75%, D5 ≥ 12/20)
```

A profile below target on a specific dimension prescribes a specific training response:

| Below-target dimension | Training response |
|---|---|
| D1 < 8 | More First-Minute Protocol drills (Ch. 4 territory); 20–40 problems with mandatory protocol logging |
| D2 < 2 | More MVC drills on 3-archetype problems (Ch. 3 + Ch. 5 territory) |
| D3 < 7 | Time-pressure simulation (mock-exam conditions); the toolkit is mature but slow |
| D4 < 75% | Decision-tree drills (Ch. 7 §1.2); the solver knows the moves but does not trigger them on the clock |
| D5 < 12 | Cross-archetype breadth practice (Pillar 2 chapter cross-references); the solver has narrow archetype habits |

The reader returns to this rubric after every 20–40 additional practice problems to re-measure the profile and re-calibrate the training response.

## §2 Worked Examples — Two graded specimens

In this section we show two specimens of the diagnostic-grading process: one problem evaluated as a high-score-high-profile attempt and one as a high-score-low-profile attempt. The contrast illustrates how the profile measures something different from the score.

### Worked Example 8.1 — Diagnostic-grading specimen (a clean attempt)

**Problem (specimen, drawn from Engel Ch. 4 territory).** Among any 5 lattice points in the plane, prove that some two have a midpoint that is also a lattice point.

**Reader's attempt (transcribed exactly).**

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------+
| 1. Given: 5 lattice points in ℤ²                             │
| 2. Find: ∃ two with integer midpoint                         │
| 3. Archetypes: #13 (pigeonhole on parity classes) + #14 (mod │
|    2 on coordinates: each point has parity-pair in 4 classes)│
| 4. Candidate CEP: 5 points, 4 parity classes ⇒ pigeonhole    │
|    forces 2 in same class ⇒ midpoint = (sum)/2 integer       │
| 5. Brute path: check all C(5,2) = 10 pairs; verifies but     │
|    doesn't generalise to "any 5 points"                      │
+--------------------------------------------------------------+
```

The reader executes the proof: 4 parity classes $\{(e,e), (e,o), (o,e), (o,o)\}$; by pigeonhole, 2 of the 5 points share a class; their sum is component-wise even, so the midpoint has integer coordinates. **Time-to-solution: 4 minutes.** No stall. Total elapsed time including Protocol: ~5 minutes.

**Grading.**

- D1 (Protocol fluency): **Y** — Step 3 named #13 + #14, which are exactly the archetypes used.
- D2 (MVC accuracy): **N-A** — 2-archetype problem; no MVC drawn (per Ch. 5 §1.4 threshold rule).
- D3 (Time discipline): **Y** — completed in 5 minutes vs. 12-minute benchmark (Tier-T2 easy).
- D4 (Escape-hatch usage): **Did-not-stall** — no escape-hatch needed.
- Contribution to D5: archetypes #13, #14.

This is a *clean attempt*: the reader's profile measurements all clearly indicate internalised discipline. The score (1/1 = pass) and the profile (all Y on relevant dimensions) are aligned.

### Worked Example 8.2 — Diagnostic-grading specimen (a pattern-match attempt)

**Problem (specimen, drawn from Engel Ch. 8 territory).** There are $n$ identical cars on a circular racetrack. The total amount of gas is exactly enough for one lap. Show that some car can complete a lap by refuelling at the other cars on its way around.

**Reader's attempt (transcribed exactly).**

```
+--------------------------------------------------------------+
| 1. Given: n cars, sum of gas = 1 lap                         │
| 2. Find: ∃ car that can complete lap                         │
| 3. Archetypes: #12 (extremal: pick the minimum-gas car? or   │
|    the maximum?)                                             │
| 4. Candidate CEP: extremal car ⇒ done somehow                │
| 5. Brute path: try each of n starting cars one by one        │
+--------------------------------------------------------------+
```

The reader's Step 3 lists only one archetype (#12). The reader then attempts the proof. After 8 minutes, the reader recalls a similar problem from Chapter 2 of Pillar 3 — *Ch. 2 Worked Example 3, the cars-on-a-circular-track problem* — and adapts that proof verbatim. The proof closes correctly at **minute 14**.

**Grading.**

- D1 (Protocol fluency): **N** — Step 3 named only one archetype; the actual proof requires #12 + #18 (extremal + induction). The reader missed the inductive structure.
- D2 (MVC accuracy): **N-A** — 2-archetype problem.
- D3 (Time discipline): **N** — completed in 14 minutes vs. 12-minute benchmark.
- D4 (Escape-hatch usage): **Grind** — the reader stalled at minute 8 but did *not* invoke a named escape-hatch; instead, the reader pattern-matched to a memorised case study and recovered by recall.
- Contribution to D5: archetypes #12, #18 (the #18 was tacit, not explicitly named in the Protocol).

This is a *pattern-match attempt*: the reader got the right answer (score: 1/1 = pass) but the profile indicates fragility (D1 = N, D4 = grind+pattern-match). On a problem *not* in the reader's case-study memory, the same approach would have failed.

The contrast between Worked Example 8.1 and 8.2 illustrates Meera Chakraborty's diagnostic insight from §0: **two readers with the same score may have radically different profiles, and the profile is what predicts future performance on novel problems.**

## §3 The Trap — The score-anxiety trap

The principal failure mode of the diagnostic instrument is *score-anxiety*: the reader focuses on the total score (number of problems solved out of 10) and ignores the profile. The score-anxious reader will, on subsequent practice, optimise for *more problems solved* rather than *better profile* — which usually means *more pattern-matching* and *less Protocol-and-MVC discipline*, because pattern-matching is faster.

The trap has a specific signature. The reader completes the diagnostic, sees a score below their expectation, and immediately resolves to *practice more problems*. They do not pause to read the profile. Within a month they have done 50 more practice problems and their score has risen by 1–2 — but their D1, D2, and D4 measurements are unchanged or have worsened. They are now *faster at pattern-matching* but no closer to the Pillar 3 discipline.

**Why does the trap happen?** Three causes:

1. *Scores are easier to track than profiles.* A single number (e.g., 6/10) is cognitively cheap; a five-dimensional profile is cognitively expensive. The default measurement attaches to the score.

2. *Indian competition culture conditions score-obsession.* JEE Main, JEE Advanced, INMO, KVPY — every Indian student preparing for olympiad mathematics has spent years in a scoring environment where the score *is* the outcome (selection, college admission). Transitioning to a profile-as-outcome model requires un-learning a deeply trained reflex.

3. *Profile improvement is slower than score improvement.* The profile measures internalised discipline — a slow-to-change quantity. The score measures problem-solving output — a faster-to-change quantity. The reader, optimising for visible improvement, naturally targets the score.

**The recovery: explicit profile tracking, ignore the score.** The Pillar 3 reader's recovery from score-anxiety is to *literally cover the score with a sticky note* after grading the diagnostic, and to read only the profile. Train against the profile-weakest dimension for 4–6 weeks; re-take the diagnostic; re-measure the profile; ignore the score; repeat. Within 12 weeks the profile is uniformly above target, and *the score follows automatically* because internalised discipline produces correct solutions as a side-effect.

(Meera Chakraborty's slide from §0 — *"Your score is the least interesting thing on this slide"* — is the canonical anti-score-anxiety prescription, repeated at every Pillar 3 training context.)

## §4 The Pillar 3 Diagnostic Set — 10 problems

The 10 problems below are calibrated as **4 Easy (T2, 2-archetype) + 4 Medium (T3, 2-to-3 borderline) + 2 Hard (T4, 3-archetype)**. Each problem is presented with the source citation, the archetype-shortlist (in italics, for self-checking after the reader's own Protocol attempt), and the benchmark time. The reader is asked to attempt each problem freshly — *no peeking at the archetype-shortlist until the Protocol scratchpad has been written*. Solutions are sealed in the Pillar 3 solutions appendix.

> ⚠ *All 10 problems below are self-selected for the diagnostic per Q6(a). Each is flagged for Anand's post-draft sign-off. Substitutions of equivalent-tier-and-archetype-profile problems are explicitly permitted.*

### EASY Tier (T2, 2-archetype, 12-minute benchmark)

**Diagnostic Problem 8.1.** *(Source: Engel Ch. 1 Problem 30 variant. Archetypes: #1 Invariance + #14 Parity.)* Twenty-five integers are written on a circle. A move consists of replacing some two adjacent integers $a, b$ with $a + 1$ and $b - 1$ (or vice versa). Prove that the sum of squares of the 25 integers is not invariant under the move, but its parity *is*.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.2.** *(Source: Zeitz §3.x. Archetypes: #13 Combinatorial + #12 Extremal.)* Show that among any 13 distinct real numbers chosen from the interval $[0, 1)$, some two are within $1/12$ of each other.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.3.** *(Source: Joshi Ch. 1 Exercise. Archetypes: #15 Bijection + #13 Combinatorial.)* How many ways can 5 boys and 5 girls be seated in a row such that no two girls are adjacent?

**Diagnostic Problem 8.4.** *(Source: Engel Ch. 4 E5 variant. Archetypes: #13 Combinatorial + #14 Parity/Modularity.)* Show that from any 7 distinct positive integers, one can choose two whose sum or difference is divisible by 11.

### MEDIUM Tier (T3, 2-to-3 borderline, 20-minute benchmark)

**Diagnostic Problem 8.5.** *(Source: Engel Ch. 8 (Induction) classical tournament theorem. Archetypes: #18 Induction + #12 Extremal + (tacit) #13 Combinatorial.)* Prove that every finite tournament (complete directed graph) contains a Hamilton path.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.6.** *(Source: Zeitz Ch. 4 territory; classical Ramsey $R(3, 3) = 6$. Archetypes: #8 Domain Translation + #13 Combinatorial + (tacit) #11 Existence.)* In any group of 6 people, prove that there are either 3 mutual acquaintances or 3 mutual strangers.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.7.** *(Source: Engel Ch. 5 functional equation. Archetypes: #5 Substitution + #11 Existence/Uniqueness.)* Find all functions $f : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ such that $f(f(n)) = n + 2$ for all positive integers $n$.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.8.** *(Source: Joshi Ch. 6 cross-reference. Archetypes: #2 Symmetry + #5 Substitution + (tacit) #4 Hidden Structure.)* Prove that for any real $x$, $\sin^2 x + \sin^2(x + 60°) + \sin^2(x + 120°) = 3/2$.

### HARD Tier (T4, 3-archetype, 30-minute benchmark)

**Diagnostic Problem 8.9.** *(Source: IMO 1972 Problem 1. Archetypes: #13 Combinatorial + #12 Extremal + #14 Parity/Modularity.)* Given any set of ten distinct two-digit positive integers, prove that one can select two disjoint subsets of this set whose elements have the same sum.

**Diagnostic Problem 8.10.** *(Source: Engel Ch. 14 §14.2 (Infinite Descent) variant. Archetypes: #14 Parity/Modularity + #16 Reverse Engineering (descent) + #18 Induction.)* Prove that there are no positive integers $a, b$ such that $a^2 + b^2 + 1 = abc$ for some positive integer $c$, *unless* the triple $(a, b, c) = (3, 3, 3)$ is the only such solution. (Variant: show $(3, 3, 3)$ is the unique solution.)

## §5 Bridge to Pillar 4

This chapter delivered the Pillar 3 closing instrument — the 10-problem diagnostic with five-dimension rubric. We named the score-anxiety trap and prescribed the cover-the-score recovery. The diagnostic, used repeatedly over 12 weeks, converts the Pillar 3 toolkit (Chs. 1–7) into reflex.

Pillar 3 is now complete. Chapters 1–4 supplied the conceptual machinery (the multidirectional thesis, the 2-archetype atom, the 3-archetype molecule, the First-Minute Protocol). Chapter 5 introduced the externalisation tool (the MVC). Chapter 6 demonstrated the full discipline on five case studies. Chapter 7 supplied the recovery engineering (the Escape-Hatch Architecture). Chapter 8 supplied the assessment instrument (this chapter). The Pillar 3 solver now has *everything they need* to attack hard problems multidirectionally — vocabulary (archetypes from Pillar 2), grammar (convergence diagrams from Ch. 1), diagnostic (Protocol from Ch. 4), externalisation (MVC from Ch. 5), demonstration (case studies from Ch. 6), recovery (escape-hatches from Ch. 7), and self-knowledge (diagnostic from Ch. 8).

**Pillar 4** — the next pillar — is structurally different. Pillars 1–3 trained the *solver*: how to read a problem, how to identify the archetypes, how to converge them, how to recover when stuck. Pillar 4 trains the *designer*: how to build a problem, how to plant the archetypes around a Central Elegant Point (CEP), how to set traps for the unwary solver, how to engineer beauty into a problem statement. The inversion is deliberate; it is the book's most original contribution, because no extant olympiad-mathematics text takes the designer's perspective seriously.

The bridge into Pillar 4 is the IMO 1988 P6 cross-reference. We treated this problem in Ch. 3 of Pillar 3 from the solver's perspective: *given* the problem, how does the solver find the Vieta-jumping proof? Pillar 4 will treat the same problem from the designer's perspective: *given* a desire to set a problem with a perfect-square CEP, why did the IMO 1988 Problem Committee choose precisely this functional form, and how were the algebraic traps planted? The two treatments are deeply complementary; the Pillar 3 solver who completes the IMO 1988 P6 proof has experienced one half of the problem; the Pillar 4 reader who reconstructs the design experiences the other half. After both halves, the problem is fully understood — and after both halves, the reader has the cognitive tools to *invent* new problems of similar elegance.
