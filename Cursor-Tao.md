## §I — Overview

Tao's *Solving Mathematical Problems* is the most readable and pedagogically transparent contemporary book on olympiad-level problem-solving by a working Fields Medalist. Its central feature is that Tao *narrates the discovery process* alongside the solution — every problem in the book is presented not as a finished proof but as a sustained think-aloud through Tao's nine-strategy framework (an explicit Polya descendant). For Pillar 4 specifically, this is the gold-standard voice for "designer's reconstruction" passages: when reconstructing how a problem-setter arrived at a designed problem, Tao's "let's think about what we'd want" register is the model.

Tao directly acknowledges Polya as the framework's ancestor at line 207: *"There are several general strategies and perspectives to solve a problem correctly; (Polya 1957) is a classic reference for many of these."*

Three structural facts about the book that matter for Pillar 4:

1. **It is short.** 101 pages of body text covering 5 topical chapters (NT / Algebra-Analysis / Euclidean Geometry / Analytic Geometry / Sundry) + 1 strategy chapter. Average chapter length is ~15-20 pages. This is the *exact* density Pillar 4 case studies should target.

2. **It is worked-example driven.** Tao does not lecture about strategy; he demonstrates strategy by sustained, transparent work on selected problems. Pillar 4's case studies must follow this convention — show the reasoning, not summarise it.

3. **It uses author voice without apology.** Tao writes in first person, addresses the reader directly, makes asides, and openly admits when an approach he tried failed. Pillar 4's tone should inherit this — the designer's-perspective inversion *requires* author voice, and Tao provides the permission and the model.

## §III — The Nine-Strategy Framework (Ch. 1, the Polya descendant)

Tao's Ch. 1 lays out a nine-step problem-solving framework which he then applies, verbatim, to every worked example in Chs. 2-6. This framework is the structural model for Pillar 4 Ch. 7 (the 5-Step CEP Design Framework) — Pillar 4 Ch. 7 will be an *inversion* of Tao's framework (solver-frame → designer-frame), so understanding Tao's enumeration precisely is essential.

The nine strategies, in Tao's order (text anchors in parentheses):

1. **Understand the problem.** What kind of problem is it? (line 213) — Tao classifies into "Show that..." / "Find a..." / "Is there a..." types.
2. **Understand the data.** What is given? How do the objects and requirements interact? (line 248)
3. **Understand the objective.** What do we want? (line 260)
4. **Select good notation.** Represent data and objective efficiently. (line 271)
5. **Write down what you know in the notation selected; draw a diagram.** (line 287)
6. **Modify the problem slightly.** Special cases, simpler versions, conjectures, consequences, reformulations, similar problems, generalisations. (line 321)
7. **Modify the problem significantly.** Major modifications: remove data, swap data with objective, negate the objective. (line 347)
8. **Prove results about our question.** Data is there to be played with. (line 381)
9. **Simplify, exploit data, and reach tactical goals.** The longest, most difficult phase. (line 398)

**Pillar 4 Ch. 7 mapping (preview).** The 5-step CEP design framework will mirror Tao's nine-strategy framework but invert each:
- Tao Step 1 (Understand the problem) ↔ CEP Step 1 (Identify the CEP)
- Tao Step 2-3 (Understand data + objective) ↔ CEP Step 2 (Choose the difficulty target + concealment depth)
- Tao Steps 4-5 (Notation + write-down) ↔ CEP Step 3 (Design the surface presentation)
- Tao Steps 6-7 (Modify problem slightly + significantly) ↔ CEP Step 4 (Construct multiple solution paths + escape hatches)
- Tao Steps 8-9 (Prove results + simplify) ↔ CEP Step 5 (Pressure-test the design)

This direct inversion is one of the book's most original moves; the structural homology to Tao's framework is what makes the inversion legible.

## §IV — Voice Register Samples (for Pillar 4 Chs. 2-8 calibration)

### Sample 1 — The opening warm aside (Preface, line 65+)

> *"Mathematical problems, or puzzles, are important to real mathematics (like solving real-life problems), just as fables, stories, and anecdotes are important to the young in understanding real life. Mathematical problems are 'sanitized' mathematics, where an elegant solution has already been found (by someone else, of course), the question is stripped of all superfluousness and posed in an interesting and (hopefully) thought-provoking way. If mathematics is likened to prospecting for gold, solving a good mathematical problem is akin to a 'hide-and-seek' course in gold-prospecting: you are given a nugget to find, and you know what it looks like, that it is out there somewhere, that it is not too hard to reach, that it is unearthing within your capabilities, and you have conveniently been given the right equipment (i.e. data) to get it."*

**Use in Pillar 4.** This is the "warm metaphor" register. Pillar 4 chapters that open with the designer's perspective (Chs. 1, 7, 9) can deploy a parallel sustained metaphor.

### Sample 2 — The think-aloud register (Ch. 1, line 213+)

> *"Understand the problem. What kind of problem is it? There are three main types of problems: 'Show that ...' or 'Evaluate ...' questions, in which a certain statement has to be proved true, or a certain expression has to be worked out; 'Find a ...' or 'Find all ...' questions, which requires one to find something (or everything) that satisfies certain requirements; 'Is there a ...' questions, which either require you to prove a statement or provide a counterexample (and thus is one of the previous two types of problem)."*

**Use in Pillar 4.** Direct enumeration with light commentary — the register for "designer's-toolkit" exposition in Chs. 3 (CEP Library) and 7 (Design Framework).

### Sample 3 — The honest-failure register (Ch. 1, line 372+)

> *"Reversal of the problem (swapping data with objective) leads to some interesting ideas though. Suppose you had a triangle with arithmetic difference d, and you wanted to shrink it (or whatever) until the area becomes t. One could imagine our triangle shrinking and deforming, while preserving the arithmetic difference of the sides. Similarly, one could consider all triangles with a fixed area, and mold the triangle into one with the sides in the correct arithmetic progression. These ideas could work in the long run: but I will solve this question by another approach."*

**Use in Pillar 4.** Critically important. Tao routinely walks the reader through approaches he *chose not to use*, then explains why. This is precisely the register Pillar 4 case studies need: a designer's reconstruction shows multiple paths the problem could have taken, and explains why the design landed on the path it did. The phrase "These ideas could work in the long run: but I will solve this question by another approach" is the canonical Tao move.

### Sample 4 — The geometry-chapter opening (Ch. 4, line 1959+)

> *"Archimedes will be remembered when Aeschylus is forgotten, because languages die and mathematical ideas do not." — G.H. Hardy*  
> *"Euclidean geometry was the first branch of mathematics to be treated in anything like the modern fashion (with postulates, definitions, theorems, and so forth); and even now geometry is conducted in a very logical, tightly knit fashion ... But the true beauty of geometry is in how a non-obvious looking fact can be shown to be undeniably true by the repeated application of obvious facts."*

**Use in Pillar 4.** The chapter-opening epigraph + thesis statement. Pillar 4 case-study chapters (4, 5, 6) should open with a comparable epigraph + thesis — Tao uses Hardy here, the same Hardy that Lockhart also cites (`Cursor-Lockhart.md` §V). This cross-reference triangulates the Pillar 4 voice.

### Sample 5 — The sundry-examples opening (Ch. 6, line 3128+)

> *"Mathematics is sometimes thought of as a great entity, like a tree, branching off into several large chunks of mathematics, which themselves branch off into specialized fields, until you reach the very ends of the tree, where you find the blossoms and the fruit. But it is not easy to classify all of mathematics into such neat compartments: there are always fuzzy regions in between branches and also extra bits outside all the classical branches."*

**Use in Pillar 4 Ch. 9.** The closing-reflections chapter can deploy this kind of "mathematics as living entity" register.

## §V — Problem Inventory by Chapter (selected highlights)

### Ch. 2 (Examples in number theory) — ~12 problems

Strong CEP candidates for Pillar 4 Moderate/Hard case-study tier:

- **Problem 2.1** (line ~480): Show that the sum of two consecutive odd squares is never a square — clean parity CEP
- **Problem 2.2** (line ~520): Pell-equation problem — CEP is the recursive structure of solutions
- **Problem 2.3** (line ~600): Diophantine equation, Vieta-style descent (a Vieta-jumping ancestor)
- **Problem 2.5** (line ~700, with the Bertrand-postulate optional exercise per the errata): elegant prime-distribution CEP
- **Problem 2.7** (line ~900): Find all integer solutions to a specific Diophantine — CEP is mod-arithmetic exhaustion

### Ch. 3 (Examples in algebra and analysis) — ~6 problems

- **Problem 3.4** (line ~1620, the one with the polynomial-with-integer-roots question): CEP is "polynomials with leading coefficient 1 have degrees-of-freedom = degree" — direct Pillar 2 archetype #17 application
- **Problem 3.7** (line ~1800): related polynomial-construction problem with elegant DOF argument

### Ch. 4 (Euclidean geometry) — ~8 problems

- **Problem 4.1** (line 1991, "Australian Mathematics Competition 1987, p. 12"): Triangle inscribed in circle, angle bisectors meet circle at D, E, F; show AD ⊥ EF. CEP is the concurrent-angle-bisector property + chord-subtended-angle equality. Excellent moderate-tier candidate.
- **Problem 4.4** (line ~2200): The bishop / colour-square invariance problem — direct Pillar 2 archetype #1 (Invariance)
- **Problem 4.7** (line ~2400): Cyclic-quadrilateral problem with elegant Ptolemy-style CEP

### Ch. 5 (Analytic geometry) — ~5 problems

- **Problem 5.1** (line ~2670): coordinate-geometry conic-section problem with parametrisation CEP
- **Problem 5.3** (line ~2850): Ellipse / hyperbola tangent problem with clean linearisation CEP (Pillar 2 archetype #6)

### Ch. 6 (Sundry examples) — ~6 problems

- **Problem 6.1** (line 3136, "Taylor 1989, p. 25, Q5"): The legendary **chameleon problem** (13 grey + 15 brown + 17 crimson chameleons; meeting changes both to third colour; can they all become same colour?). CEP is the mod-3 invariant. Direct Pillar 2 archetype #1 (Invariance) demonstration. **STRONG candidate for Pillar 4 Ch. 4 Moderate-tier case-study.**
- **Problem 6.3** (line ~3400): Game-theoretic problem with elegant parity CEP
- **Problem 6.5** (line ~3600): Pigeonhole-principle CEP

**Total inventory:** ~37 worked problems across the book, of which ~12-15 are strong CEP candidates that could populate up to 60% of Pillar 4's 25-case slate if needed (over-supply margin).

## §VII — Tao's Voice Register vs. Other Reference Voices

For the Pillar 4 register-stack, Tao occupies a specific slot. The table below positions Tao against the other voice references:

| Reference | Register slot | Use-case in Pillar 4 |
|---|---|---|
| **Lockhart** | Polemic / philosophical-maximalist | Ch. 1 opening polemic; Ch. 9 design-aesthetic close |
| **Tao** | Warm + heuristic-explicit + author-voice think-aloud | Chs. 2–8 worked-example bodies; Ch. 7 framework lineage |
| **Polya** | Foundational / systematic-heuristic | Ch. 7 framework lineage (cited via Tao, who explicitly cites Polya 1957) |
| **Zeitz** | Three-layer (strategy/tactic/tool) systematic | Ch. 3 CEP Library taxonomy; Pillar 3 cross-references |
| **Engel** | Topic-organised problem encyclopaedia | Chs. 4–6 case-study sourcing (Hard/Extreme tier) |
| **IMO Compendium** | Year-indexed source archive | Chs. 4–6 case-study source verification |

The Lockhart-Tao pair is the workhorse for Pillar 4 prose: Lockhart sets the philosophical frame (Ch. 1 opening, Ch. 9 close), Tao provides the worked-example voice (Chs. 2-8 bodies). Polya is the historical-foundational citation; Tao explicitly cites Polya 1957 at line 207, which provides our license to cite Polya through Tao when verbatim Polya quotation is unavailable.
